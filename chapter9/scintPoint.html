Introduction

Listing 9.8 in my book provides an example simulation of propagating a point source to an observation plane. That example propagates through many random draws of the turbulent phase screens and computes the spatial covariance of the complex optical fields in the observation plane. Each covariance is stored, and the average (normalized to a peak value of 1 at zero separation) is the coherence factor. The theoretical coherence factor is given in various forms by Eqs. (9.37), (9.38), and (9.44)-(9.47). Figure 9.10 shows a close comparison between the simulated cohrence factor and the theoretical one from the combination of Eqs. (9.32) and (9.44). This is presented as verification that the simulation works correctly. This is not the only statistic that can be checked.

Scintillation is another useful property of turbulent simulations to check. The term scintillation refers to fluctuations in the amplitude/log-amplitude/irradiance/log-irradiance of an optical field. Specifically, this article refers to the same observation plane as in the the example from Listing 9.8. There is no optical system, nor image in this example. Later, this article presents an augmented version of Listing 9.8 that checks multiple scintillation probability density functions (PDFs).

Scintillation in Weak Turbulence Theory

The reader should recall from Eq.\ (9.25) that in the Rytov method, the optical field is written as $$ \begin{equation} U\left(\mathbf{r}\right) \simeq U_0\left(\mathbf{r}\right)\exp\left[\psi\left(\mathbf{r}\right)\right], \end{equation} $$ where \(U_0\left(\mathbf{r}\right)\) is the unperturbed field and \(\psi\left(\mathbf{r}\right)\) is the complex perturbation due to turbulence. Also, $$ \begin{equation} \psi = \chi + i\phi, \end{equation} $$ where \(\chi\) is the log-amplitude perturbation and \(\phi\) is the phase perturbation. Equation (9.64) provides the spherical-wave log-amplitude variance \(\sigma_{\chi,sw}^2\) at a point. Readers should bear in mind that Eq. (9.64) is valid only in weak turbulence for which \(\sigma_{\chi,sw}^2 \lesssim 0.25\). This expression is often called the Rytov number. In weak turbulence, the irradiance variance is given by $$ \begin{equation} \sigma_I^2 \simeq 4\,\sigma_{\chi}^2. \end{equation} $$ I have omitted the subscript for indicating a spherical or planar wave because this factor of 4 applies in both cases. The equation for \(\sigma_I^2\) is often called the Rytov variance.

In the Rytov method, the perturbations \(\chi\) and \(\phi\) are treated as having Gaussian PDFs. To be specific for the log-amplitude, $$ \begin{equation} p\left(\chi\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma_{\chi}^2}} \exp\left[-\frac{\left(\chi-\mu_{\chi}\right)^2}{2\sigma_{\chi}^2}\right], \end{equation} $$ Moreover, the mean log-amplitude perturbation is given by $$ \begin{equation} \mu_{\chi} = -\sigma_{\chi}^2. \end{equation} $$ The amplitude perturbation can be defined as $$ \begin{equation} A = \exp{\chi}. \end{equation} $$ With this random variable transformation, the amplitude has a log-normal PDF given by $$ \begin{equation} p\left(A\right) = \frac{1}{A\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma_{\chi}^2}} \exp\left[-\frac{\left(\ln A-\mu_{\chi}\right)^2}{2\sigma_{\chi}^2}\right]. \end{equation} $$

It is customary to work with the normalized irradiance, $$ \begin{equation} \widehat{I} = \frac{I}{\mu_I} = \frac{\vert U \vert^2}{< \vert U \vert^2 >} = \frac{A^2}{< A^2 >}, \end{equation} $$ where \(\mu_I\) is the mean irradiance.

Scintillation in Strong Turbulence Theory

There are multiple strong turbulence theories. For example, the Extended Huygens-Fresnel Principle can be used to predict properties like the mean irradiance, which is a second moment of the field. Extended Rytov theory is used to model fourth moments of the field, like the scintillation variance and spatial covariance. Here, I provide a short summary of the equations used to compute the scintillation variance of a spherical wave in Extended Rytov theory.

Extended Rytov theory is two-scale model of optical turbulence. This means that the overall irradiance fluctuation is modeled as the product of two statistically independent factors such that $$ \begin{equation} I = X Y, \end{equation} where \(X\) is the large-scale fluctuation and \(Y\) is the small-scale fluctuation.
